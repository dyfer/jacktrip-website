Why is Latency Important?

The Internet is widely used for audio communications. Numerous collaboration applications exist that make it trivial to carry on a conversation with almost anyone, worldwide. Likely, you already use these applications regularly. What is thought of as traditional phone lines have even largely migrated towards running over the Internet. So, why is performing music any different?

The answer lies in the problem of trying to keep a common rhythm together. Maintaining a shared beat or sense of pulse is difficult if it takes too long for one musician's sound to reach another's ears. There can be drastic consequences. The "Happy Birthday" effect is familiar from video conference apps. The length of time for sound to get around the group makes it impossible for the group to sing together. Individuals find themselves in a situation of "I'm waiting for you and you're waiting for me" and that's the problem. Video calls are engineered for talking and turn taking. Music is different because it involves simultaneous coordinated activity rather than alternation. Tightly synchronized performance only works over the Internet with specially-adapted applications like JackTrip.

A group’s ability to maintain a steady pulse is heavily impacted by what is known as latency. This is a term which refers to how long it takes for one performer's sound to reach the ears of another. It is typically measured in milliseconds, or 1/1000 of a second. Research has found that the ability to perform syncronized rhythms together requires a latency below 25-30 milliseconds. There isn’t a hard and fast number for this because everyone is different and musical situations are different. Particularly important in this regard is the speed or tempo of a piece (measured in beats per minute). Slower tempi can tolerate relatively longer latencies.

To help put this into perspective, sound traveling 25 feet (roughly 8 meters) through air takes about 25 milliseconds. We're comfortable playing or singing together at distances around this range. As a group spreads out, say across a football field, its ability to maintain a coordinate rhythm becomes increasingly difficult. This is why minimizing latency is so important.

Optimizing Latency

When performing music over the Internet, the sound you make has to pass through several stages to reach another performer. Each of these stages adds latency. We’ve color-coded these to help illustrate the relative speed of each stage, with slow being orange and the slowest red. The Internet backbone stage is blue because it is special.

Neutral

The Internet backbone latency

  • Geographical distance between performers

Geography has the largest impact on the latency of the Internet backbone. Typically, the farther away one performer is from another, the longer it will take the sound to reach them. This is limited not just by the speed of light, but also how many different backbone networks (or “network hops”) it has to pass through. This limitation means you are likely unable to experience low latency with anyone that is physically located more than a few hundred miles away from you. But the Internet backbone is usually very fast (a few milliseconds) if your physical distance from other performers is less than a few hundred miles.

High Level

Internet connection latency

  • The connection between your home and the Internet
  • The connection between you and the other performer’s home and the Internet

Internet connection latency depends on what type of you have, with fiber-to-the-home (FTTH) being fastest (about 2 milliseconds) and cable being one of the slowest (about 10-15 milliseconds). Note that latency is very different from the bandwidth of your Internet connection, which is how much data it is able to transmit over a given period of time. Having a high bandwidth rate (even gigabit) often does not correspond to having low latency. Given that the Internet connection to your home is not easy to change, and the availability of low latency connections is relatively poor, we will focus on optimizing latency for the other stages.

Medium Level

Home network latency

  • Your home network
  • The other performer’s home network

The home network is the easiest to optimize. Quite simply, do not use WiFi. Even the best WiFi routers add significant latency and jitter (the range of latency over time). Connect the computer or device you are using directly into your home network using an Ethernet cable. This will reduce latency for the home network stage to less than 1 millisecond.

Medium Level

ADC and DAC latency in computers

  • An analog-to-digital (ADC) electronics in your computer
  • A digital-to-analog (DAC) electronics in the other performer’s computer

The latency from ADC and DAC are determined by the hardware you are using, often referred to as your “sound card.” Every sound card is different and our testing of various devices indicates a very large range. Laptops can easily have latency over 100 milliseconds, and even the best USB hardware has latency in the range of 5-15 milliseconds. Low latency is often not a high priority for sound card manufacturers, because the most common use cases like playing back recorded music, music video soundtracks, video games, and conversations, do not require it.

The best way to reduce your sound card latency is using a hardware device which is specifically designed for it. The most economical way we’ve found to do this is using our JackTrip Device, which includes a Raspberry Pi computer bundled with HiFiBerry sound card. It provides very high-quality audio at a low price, while also enabling you to reduce this latency to about 1 millisecond.

Low Level

Audio hardware latency

  • The air between you and your microphone
  • Your microphone electronics and cabling
  • The other performer’s headphone or speaker electronics and cabling
  • The air between the other performer and their headphones or speakers

To keep latency low, it is recommended to use analog microphones and headphones with your setup.

Understanding the Technology Models

When performing music over the There are two technology models commonly available today for the transmission of audio (including music) over the Internet: the client-server model, and the peer-to-peer (P2P) model. Let’s explore the differences of each model.

The Peer-to-Peer Model

In the Peer-to-Peer (or P2P) model, each performer’s computer sends a copy of their audio input directly to every other performer. A performer’s computer mixes all the audio streams together and plays the result to their audio output. You can visualize this model as a mesh pattern:

Peer-to-Peer Model

Pros:

  • Does not require a central server
  • Has the potential for lowest latency

Cons:

  • Has high processing requirements on each performer’s computer, which typically have very limited capacity
  • Has high upload and download bandwidth requirements for each performer’s home Internet connection
  • Requires changes in each performer’s home Internet firewall to forward ports to their computer

It’s important to note that the processing and bandwidth requirements on each performer’s computer for the P2P model are directly proportional to the number of performers. Even the most powerful laptops and the best home Internet connections cannot handle more than about five performers. This model is not viable for larger groups.

The Client Server Model

In the Client-Server Model, every performer’s computer sends a single copy of their audio input to a central server. The server mixes all the audio streams together and sends a single copy of the mix back to every performer’s computer, which plays it to their audio output. The Client-Server Model

Pros:

  • Only very rarely requires any changes in the performers’ home Internet firewalls
  • Has minimal upload, download and processing requirements for the performers computers and home Internet connections

Cons:

  • Requires configuration and management of a central server
  • Adds latency through an additional stage in the audio path (the server)

The processing and bandwidth requirements for each performer in the client-server model remain constantly low, regardless of the number of performers. However, this is not completely free. The server’s processing and bandwidth requirements still grow proportionally with the number of performers. The key difference is that servers are designed for this, and can easily be scaled to handle up to handle hundreds of performers. This makes the client-server model most suitable for use with groups that are larger than a handful of performers.
