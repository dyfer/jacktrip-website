Why is Latency Important?

The Internet is widely used for audio communications. Numerous collaboration applications exist that make it trivial to carry on a conversation with almost anyone, worldwide. Likely, you already use these applications regularly. What is thought of as traditional phone lines have even largely migrated towards running over the Internet. So, why is performing music any different?

The answer lies in the problem of trying to keep a common rhythm together. Maintaining a shared beat or sense of pulse is difficult if it takes too long for one musician's sound to reach another's ears. There can be drastic consequences. The "Happy Birthday" effect is familiar from video conference apps. The length of time for sound to get around the group makes it impossible for the group to sing together. Individuals find themselves in a situation of "I'm waiting for you and you're waiting for me" and that's the problem. Video calls are engineered for talking and turn taking. Music is different because it involves simultaneous coordinated activity rather than alternation. Tightly synchronized performance only works over the Internet with specially-adapted applications like JackTrip.

A group’s ability to maintain a steady pulse is heavily impacted by what is known as latency. This is a term which refers to how long it takes for one performer's sound to reach the ears of another. It is typically measured in milliseconds, or 1/1000 of a second. Research has found that the ability to perform syncronized rhythms together requires a latency below 25-30 milliseconds one way. There isn’t a hard and fast number for this because everyone is different and musical situations are different. Particularly important in this regard is the speed or tempo of a piece (measured in beats per minute). Slower tempi can tolerate relatively longer latencies.

To help put this into perspective, sound traveling 25 feet (roughly 8 meters) through air takes about 25 milliseconds. We're comfortable playing or singing together at distances around this range. As a group spreads out, say across a football field, its ability to maintain a coordinated rhythm becomes increasingly difficult. This is why minimizing latency is so important.

Optimizing Latency

When performing music over the Internet, the sound you make has to pass through several stages to reach another performer. Each of these stages adds latency.

Neutral

Geographical latency and Internet backbone

  • Geographical distance between performers
  • Internet paths and hops

The farther away one performer is from another, the longer it will take the sound to travel between them. The globe is laced with fiberoptic cables running across land and under the sea. Connected together, these form the Internet. Data travels near the speed-of-light across these “backbone” network segments and transits from one to another at relay points, or “network hops”. The greater the distance and the greater the number of hops the longer a sound takes to get from source to destination. Good rhythmically tight latencies are achievable when musicians are physically located no more than a few hundred miles apart. A dozen milliseconds to go across a large metropolitan area one way is typical.

High Level

Internet service latency

  • Connection from home to Internet
  • Service plans

Each musician's home or studio has an Internet connection. Latency depends on the type of connection with fiber-to-the-home (FTTH) being fastest (about 2 milliseconds) and cable or DSL being among the slowest (about 10-15 milliseconds). Connection latency is not the same as the bandwidth of your Internet connection which specifies how much data can be transmitted over a given period of time. Having a high bandwidth rate (even gigabit) may not correspond to having low latency. The typical tools to measure quality-of-service (QoS) of a connection are speed test and round-trip ping time (to a test point elsewhere on the Internet). However, these only give a rough approximation of the QoS required for network music performance. What matters most for latency is the smoothness of very fast data flows (audio packet jitter). This is best when connections are uncongested.

Medium Level

Home network latency

  • Wired only (not WiFi)

Quite simply, do not use WiFi. Even the best WiFi routers add significant latency and an impossible amount of jitter (occasionally stalling audio packet flows for 10's of milliseconds). Computers or devices being used for JackTrip need to be connected to the home network using an Ethernet cable (usually plugged into ethernet ports in the back of the WiFi router). Latency across the wired portions of a home network can be less than 1 millisecond.

Medium Level

ADC and DAC latency in computers

  • Analog-to-digital (ADC) electronics in your computer
  • Digital-to-analog (DAC) electronics in the other performer’s computer

The latency from ADC and DAC are determined by the hardware you are using, often referred to as your “sound card” or audio interface. Every sound card is different and there's a very large range. Laptops can easily have latencies over 100 milliseconds, and even the best USB-based audio interfaces have latencies in the range of 5-15 milliseconds (total in / out). Low latency is more of a priority for sound card manufacturers targeting music studio applications, rather than use cases like music players, video players, gaming, and dialog recording. Unfortunately, USB microphones are not yet proving to be low enough, either.

The best way to reduce sound card latency is using a hardware device which is designed for minimal latency. An economical way we’ve found is what's bundled in our Raspberry Pi JackTrip Device, which includes a HiFiBerry sound card. Latency is exceptionally good at about 1 millisecond.

Low Level

Acoustic latency

  • The distance to your microphone and loudspeakers.
  • The distance between the other performer and their microphone and loudspeakers

Bluetooth devices are out, analog only. Remembering that the speed of sound in air is significant (1 millisecond per foot), care should be taken to keep the microphone close to the sound source. Analog headphones are obviously great for proximity but loudspeakers can also be used, bearing in mind that they should be in near proximity (while avoiding the possibility of feedback). Electronic instruments (guitars, keyboards, etc.) can be plugged directly into the analog input of the audio interface. Monitor their sound by mixing it to the headphones or loudspeakers via the device's direct monitoring.

Understanding the Technology Models

When performing music over the There are two technology models commonly available today for the transmission of audio (including music) over the Internet: the client-server model, and the peer-to-peer (P2P) model. Let’s explore the differences of each model.

The Peer-to-Peer Model

In the Peer-to-Peer (or P2P) model, each performer’s computer sends a copy of their audio input directly to every other performer. A performer’s computer mixes all the audio streams together and plays the result to their audio output. You can visualize this model as a mesh pattern:

Peer-to-Peer Model

Pros:

  • Does not require a central server
  • Has the potential for lowest latency

Cons:

  • Has high processing requirements on each performer’s computer, which typically have very limited capacity
  • Has high upload and download bandwidth requirements for each performer’s home Internet connection
  • Requires changes in each performer’s home Internet firewall to forward ports to their computer

It’s important to note that the processing and bandwidth requirements on each performer’s computer for the P2P model are directly proportional to the number of performers. Even the most powerful laptops and the best home Internet connections cannot handle more than about five performers. This model is not viable for larger groups.

The Client Server Model

In the Client-Server Model, every performer’s computer sends a single copy of their audio input to a central server. The server mixes all the audio streams together and sends a single copy of the mix back to every performer’s computer, which plays it to their audio output. The Client-Server Model

Pros:

  • Only very rarely requires any changes in the performers’ home Internet firewalls
  • Has minimal upload, download and processing requirements for the performers computers and home Internet connections

Cons:

  • Requires configuration and management of a central server
  • Adds latency through an additional stage in the audio path (the server)

The processing and bandwidth requirements for each performer in the client-server model remain constantly low, regardless of the number of performers. However, this is not completely free. The server’s processing and bandwidth requirements still grow proportionally with the number of performers. The key difference is that servers are designed for this, and can easily be scaled to handle up to handle hundreds of performers. This makes the client-server model most suitable for use with groups that are larger than a handful of performers.
